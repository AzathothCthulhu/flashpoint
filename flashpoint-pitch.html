The Problem
Tabletop exercises are training products
wearing a game's costume.
"The exercise was excellent."
— Every post-exercise survey. Regardless of outcome. Regardless of what changed afterwards.
01
Players know the answer exists
The facilitator has a script. Everyone in the room knows it. Real crisis decisions do not come with a pre-agreed correct answer waiting to be revealed.
02
Stakes aren't real
When nothing materially changes based on your decision, you don't feel the decision. You answer the question. That is not the same thing as making the call under pressure.
03
Evaluation anxiety destroys honesty
Participants perform competence rather than exercise judgment. The wrong learning happens in a room full of people trying not to look bad in front of leadership.
04
Forgettable by design
Without emotional stakes and narrative tension, retention is measured in days. The compliance checkbox gets ticked. The organisation is not actually more prepared.

INTEL New information arrives. Always fragmentary, always asymmetric. The CISO sees something Legal doesn't. What players share — and when — is itself a consequential decision.
INTERPRET Players must synthesise partial information under time pressure. Organisational dynamics play out. Who trusts whom? What gets withheld? The game creates conditions — players create outcomes.
DECIDE A decision gate arrives. Resource allocation. Notification timing. Public statement. Made with incomplete information under real time pressure. No safety net.
CONSEQUENCE The decision changes game state — not by facilitator intervention, but by mechanics. Delay notification and dwell time compounds. Resource pools shift. The world responds to decisions, not to intentions.
ESCALATE The world does not wait. New injects arrive conditioned on the current state. Respond well: escalation is lateral. Respond poorly: it compounds. The loop repeats with higher stakes.
Core Mechanic — 2 of 4
The Resource Economy
Five pools represent the organisation's operational health. Decisions deplete and restore them. The cascade when any one runs out is what makes risk appetite stop being an abstract concept.
TIME
The only non-renewable resource. Runs in real-time during sessions. Every decision, investigation, and communication costs it. Cannot be recovered. Models the brutal arithmetic of real incidents.
Depleting → Capacity degrades → Decision quality falls → Cascade accelerates
CAPACITY
Staff hours available for response. Allocate people to workstreams. Under-staff a stream and it degrades. Models the "we only have two people" reality most security teams live in.
Depleted by time pressure and poor prioritisation
REPUTATION
Public and stakeholder trust. Degraded by poor communications, regulatory failures, visible gaps between what was said and what happened. Hard to recover once lost.
Currently critical — delayed public statement, media coverage compounding
REGULATORY STANDING
Your position with regulators. Starts neutral. Improves through proactive notification and transparency. Degrades through delay, obfuscation, or incomplete disclosure. Determines tolerance for future errors.
Regulator flexibility is conditional on this score
INTELLIGENCE QUALITY
How accurate your picture of the threat actually is. Degrades without investigation. Improves with correct attribution and analysis. Directly affects quality of every downstream decision.
Low IQ means all decisions are made on wrong assumptions
The cascade principle: Running out of TIME damages CAPACITY. Low REPUTATION reduces regulator goodwill and depletes REGULATORY STANDING. Poor INTELLIGENCE QUALITY means DECIDE phase outcomes are less predictable. The resources are not independent — they are a system.
Core Mechanic — 3 of 4
Information Asymmetry & Role Architecture
Each player holds a Role Packet — information only they receive. Three layers: what their role knows, what their character was told, what they personally need. Optionally: a Fault Line Card — a conflicting interest that creates realistic organisational friction without making anyone the villain.

Core Mechanic — 4 of 4
The Escalation Engine
Not A Script. A State Machine.
The scenario runs on player decisions, not a facilitator's script. Every decision moves game state. The next inject is conditioned on the current state. The facilitator reads the machine — they do not author the story.
Compound Consequences
Delayed notification increases dwell time → regulatory exposure window grows → Regulatory Standing depletes → regulator becomes less forgiving when the next mistake arrives. Players feel the cascade. That is the lesson — not the slide that explains it afterwards.
Three Endings. None Clean.
There is no pass or fail. Three different consequence states — each with different costs, different lessons, different debrief conversations. The best ending requires near-perfect play. The worst teaches the most. Players come away wanting to know what the other endings felt like.

Design Principle
The Narrative Layer gives mechanics meaning.
🏢
An Organisation With a Soul
Not "TechCorp Ltd." A company with history, culture, internal politics, and people who have names and feel real. Players must want this organisation to survive the scenario — not merely solve it.
👤
A Human Protagonist
Not the player characters — a character in the scenario world affected by the crisis. The IT administrator who made an honest mistake. The patient whose records are exposed. Their fate is woven through the mechanics.
⚖️
A Moral Question
Not a lesson — a question. "How much transparency does an organisation owe when the truth is uncertain?" "When does following the rules become the wrong thing to do?" The mechanics enforce the decision. The story makes players care about the answer.
🌍
A World With Consequence
The CEO is on holiday and unreachable. The intern sent the phishing email because they were never trained. The vendor sold a product with a known vulnerability. These details affect game state — and they are also story.
The mechanics enforce the decision. The story makes players care about the answer. This is what makes a FLASHPOINT scenario memorable — not the technical challenge, but the human weight of getting it wrong.
Scenario Architecture
"Third Party" — Complete Scenario Specification
The Organisation
Meridian Health — a mid-sized private hospital group that migrated to NovaCare's patient management system three months ago to reduce costs.
The Trigger Event
A security researcher contacts Meridian claiming to have found patient records for sale on a dark web forum. The data format matches NovaCare exactly. NovaCare denies any breach. The researcher is publishing in 48 hours regardless of Meridian's response.
The Human Element
Buried in the exposed data is Dr. Sarah Obi — a Meridian cardiologist who is also a patient there, receiving treatment for a mental health condition she has not disclosed to her employer. She doesn't know. She is at work today.
The Moral Question
Transparency requires notifying Dr. Obi and all affected patients. But doing so before breach confirmation risks destroying NovaCare — 40 employees, potentially innocent. The NDB says notify at "likely." When does the evidence reach that threshold?
Resource Pressure
TIME: researcher publishes in 48h  ·  CAPACITY: Meridian has 2 security staff  ·  INTELLIGENCE: NovaCare is stonewalling  ·  REPUTATION: every hour without notification is compounding liability  ·  REGULATORY: NDB awareness clock is contested
THREE ENDINGS
TRANSPARENT & DAMAGING
Players notify patients early. Dr. Obi finds out at work. NovaCare is later exonerated — breach was a third-party aggregator. Meridian did the right thing. It cost them. What does that teach?
CONTAINED & COMPROMISED
Players delay notification, technically within regulatory limits. The researcher publishes. Regulatory investigation follows. Meridian survives — but in what condition, and what version of "survive" is this?
THE RIGHT CALL
Players solve attribution correctly, notify patients with appropriate framing, work with NovaCare to contain the aggregator. Hard, expensive, technically demanding. Requires near-perfect play across all mechanics. This is what "excellent" actually looks like.
